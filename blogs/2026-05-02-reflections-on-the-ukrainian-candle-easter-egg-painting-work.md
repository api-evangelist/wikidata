<p>On Wednesday, 8 April 2026, I had the privilege of attending a Ukrainian Candle Easter Egg Painting Workshop at City University in Cyberjaya, Malaysia, as one of the Wikimedians representing <a href="https://meta.wikimedia.org/wiki/Wikimedia_Community_User_Group_Malaysia">Wikimedia Community User Group Malaysia</a> (WCUGM). It was hosted by the Embassy of Ukraine in Malaysia, supported by the EU Delegation in Malaysia.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Pysanky as a tradition</h2>



<p>This workshop was an immersive introduction to <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Egg_decorating_in_Slavic_culture">pysanky</a>, one of Ukraine&#8217;s oldest and most symbolically rich artistic traditions, and an occasion to consider the cultural resilience that sustains such practices across centuries of history.</p>



<p>The word&nbsp; (plural: pysanky) derives from the Ukrainian verb pysaty, meaning <a href="https://crees.ku.edu/pysanky">to write</a>. Rather than being decorated in any casual or ornamental sense, pysanky are, in the truest meaning of the word, written upon. Each line and motif constitutes an element of a deliberate visual language whose origins lie in pre-Christian Ukrainian folk culture. The <a href="https://ich.unesco.org/en/RL/pysanka-ukrainian-tradition-and-art-of-decorating-eggs-02134">tradition</a> considerably predates the Christianisation of Ukraine and was subsequently <a href="https://heritagecrafts.org.uk/craft/pysanka-ukrainian-tradition-and-art-of-decorating-eggs/">absorbed into Easter observance</a>, within which it has endured as a central and abiding expression of seasonal and spiritual meaning.</p>

<p>Before the workshop started, we were provided with a presentation of the history and background of the pysanky tradition. It was an illuminating aspect of the workshop, as we learned the <a href="https://thepysankashop.wordpress.com/2017/04/14/motifs-designs-in-pysanky/">symbolic</a> vocabulary underpinning pysanky design. Far from being arbitrary ornamentation, each motif carries established meaning, the product of generations of cultural transmission and refinement. For example, the sun is among the oldest motifs in pysanky, representing life and light. The Infinite, depicted through a wave motif, serves as a protective symbol believed to trap and ward off evil. Oak leaves are emblems of masculine strength and endurance.</p>



<p><a href="https://ukrainianeggcessories.com/pages/the-symbolism-of-colour-on-your-pysanka?srsltid=AfmBOoq8B7gLNMBuVmMCW-jV1CUaRkOe_4dMyL7ckdKV45fExSfKLgpj">Colour</a>, too, operates as a <a href="https://www.archaeologynow.org/blog/the-ancient-story-of-pysanky">carrier of meaning</a> within this tradition. Red signifies life and vitality; black — contrary to its associations in many Western contexts—represents the fertility of the earth and an honouring of those who have passed; yellow evokes the generosity of the harvest sun; and green speaks to the renewal of growth. A single pysanka constitutes an entire statement of intent and aspiration for its tradition.</p>

<p>My own completed egg bore an eight-pointed flower at its centre, flanked by four diamond shapes which I painted in gold and pink, each framing a grey interior. According to the <a href="https://crees.ku.edu/pysanky#:~:text=Color%20Symbolism,Respect%2C%20protection%20from%20evil%20powers">symbolic grammar</a> of pysanky, the composition expressed a wish for enlightenment, abundance, and protection. The embossed surface of the candle wax added a further tactile dimension. The raised patterns caught and reflected the candlelight in a manner that felt wholly appropriate to the tradition. For instance, the flower shape in the middle of the egg candle is known as <a href="https://crees.ku.edu/pysanky#:~:text=Color%20Symbolism,Respect%2C%20protection%20from%20evil%20powers">Ruzha</a>, which represents the Sun God, Dazhboh.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading">The painting workshop</h2>



<p>Participants were introduced to the candle easter egg that was crafted by the wife of the Ukrainian Ambassador in Malaysia. She handmade the egg candle out of soy wax and used a candle mold in two designs. I personally chose the bigger egg candle with the geometric motif embossed to paint.</p>



<p>The process proceeds with me trying to paint the egg candle in careful sequence, from the lightest colour to the darkest—gold, pink, then yellow, green, red, and finally brown. That was a technique that required not only patience and precision, but a willingness to trust a process because the results remain invisible until the very last moment.</p>



<p>The workshop was modest in setting but significant in its delivery. It offered participants an opportunity to engage directly with this tradition. Over 20 people from diverse backgrounds took part, and the focus in the room reflected just how much this kind of hands-on cultural experience resonates with people. The WCUGM also took the moment to promote an upcoming event, Ukraine&#8217;s Cultural Diplomacy Month 2026 Edit-a-thon, happening on 23rd April 2026 at the same venue.</p>



<p>Finally, although I do not consider myself to be artistic, to sit with a stylus, water colour, and an egg candle, and to attempt, however humbly, to participate in a practice that stretches back millennia was a genuinely instructive experience. To paint a pysanka is to add one&#8217;s voice to a conversation of peace that has been ongoing for thousands of years.</p>
